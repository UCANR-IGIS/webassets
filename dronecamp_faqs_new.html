Course Content

What makes DroneCamp special?

Creating 2D and 3D mapping products from drone imagery requires a relatively large number of skillsets. While there are numerous classes out there that cover regulations, flying, and/or data processing, there aren't that many programs that cover the full suite of skills (especially data processing and analytics). DroneCamp covers entire workflow, and will connect you with researchers and people just like yourself interested in converting raw data into actionable information for management and research.

Plus it's a lot of fun!

Will I learn how to fly a drone?

If you'd like to - yes! There will be an optional "pre-DroneCamp" workshop on basic flying on Monday June 22, designed for those with little to no experience with drones. On Wednesday June 24, there will be two workshops on more advanced flight skills, including risk assessment, situational awareness, crew coordination, emergency procedures, and autonomous flight.

Do I need a drone?

No. If you sign up for one of the flight sessions, drones will be provided. If you have your own drone, you are welcome to bring it for the equipment show-n-tell, but please let the organizers know in advance.

Which drones will we use?

Flight practice will be with small quadcopters, including the DJI Phantom III Standard and 3DR Solo. Other UAVs that will be displayed and/or demonstrated including the DJI Inspire 1, Matrice 100, Matrice 200, eBee (fixed wing), and others.

Do I need a laptop?

No. The stitching and data analysis exercises will be held in a computer lab with all the necessary software installed. If you'd like to use your own laptop for a workshop, see the workshop description for details on the type and software required.

Will we learn how to process RGB images or multi-spectral?

Both.

Which software will we use?

There will be workshops for stitching drone images using Pix4Dmapper, Agisoft Metashape, and ArcGIS Pro. Analysis workshops will use ArcGIS Pro and open source tools. See the workshop descriptions for details.

Will this course allow me to legally fly drones for my work?

The short answer is no. To legally fly a drone for any other non-recreational purpose (with the possible exception of students doing research), you must hold a remote pilot license from the FAA (aka Part 107). This requires passing a written exam. There will be a workshop at DroneCamp on Part 107 regulations, including some of the more challenging topics on the Part 107 exam (including airspace charts). This will help you prepare for the exam, but you'll still need to study and take the exam at a FAA approved test center.

Will the night-flying workshop allow me to legally fly a drone at night?

Short answer: generally yes for students and employees of the University of California who have their Part 107, and generally no for everyone else.

Long answer: Flying at night requires a waiver from the FAA. The night flying workshop at DroneCamp will satisfy the training requirement to fly at night if you are covered by the University of California's night flying Certificate of Authorization (COA) from the FAA. That means you if take the workshop, and if you are covered by the UC COA (all UC students and employees are elligble), and if you have your Part 107 license, and if you install the required lighting equipment on your drone and follow all the rules, then you can fly legally at night.

If you belong to an organization that does not have a night flying COA with the FAA, you can still take the workshop to find out what a night-flying COA is and how to get one.

Format and Workshop Materials

Can I take all the workshops?

Unfortunately no. In general there will be 3-4 workshops running concurrently so you'll have to pick one to attend. Workshops that are in high demand (based on a preferences survey) might be offered a second time to accommodate more people.

Can I attend workshops in different tracks?

Yes, you can mix and match workshops based on your interests and the schedule.

Do I have to pre-register for workshops?

The only workshops that require pre-reigstration are the flight practice sessions on Monday and Wednesday, which have a maximum number of people that can be accommodated. For the data processing and analysis workshops, we anticipate there will be enough workstations in the two computer labs, but if needed people can pair-up.

Will workshops be recorded?

There are no plans to video record workshops. However workshop materials such as slides, handouts, and exercises will be available to everyone through the DroneCamp website.

Travel and Logistics

Where do we go?

The classroom sessions will be at Cal State University Monterey Bay in the Joel and Dena Gambord Business and Information Technology Building. Flight practice will be at the Fort Ord Reserve. More details and maps will be provided during the program.

How about parking?

On the CSUMB campus, several parking lots are within a 5 minute walk of the Business and Information Technology Building where the classroom sessions will be held (see Campus Parking Maps). Daily parking permits are available for $5/day at dispensers or online. Online purchase is recommended, as you can print your pass at home.

Parking at the flight practice area at Fort Ord Reserve is limited and will be reserved for those with mobility issues and group vehicles. However there is ample parking just outside the gate which is a pleasant 15 minute walk from the launch area.

What's the nearest airport?

Monterey Regional Airport is nearby, but relatively small with limited service. The nearst large airports are San Jose, San Francisco, and Oakland. Monterey Airbus has shuttle service to San Jose and SFO on a fixed schedule. For public transit, take CalTrain or Amtrak to the San Jose Diridon Station, then the 17 Express from San Jose to Santa Cruz, followed by the MST #78 bus from Santa Cruz to Monterey. See also the CSUMB Campus Transit Map.

Is accommodation provided? Where should we stay?

Accommodation is not provided. There are a number of hotels around Monterey. Check back soon for some options.

How about camping?

A limited number of tent camping spots with basic facilities are available at UCSC Fort Ord Natural Reserve (the same location as the flight practice sessions). A survey link to apply for a camping spot will be provided in the confirmation email after registration. Camping sites will be allocated on a first-come, first-served basis. Once all spots are taken, an announcement will be made at the top of the DroneCamp web page. Camping is being offered by FONR as a free public service for DroneCamp participants.
